Climate Data and Crime Rates in the United States

For our final project, we decided to map and compare Temperature, Precipitation, Crime Rates, and FEMA Declarations by state.

  • Using the dropdown menu, you can view each dataset in choropleth form.
  • VCR: Violent Crimes per 100,000 people
  • PCR: Property Crimes per 100,000 people
  • FEMA: FEMA declarations by state per year
  • TEMP: Yearly average temperature by state in degrees Fahrenheit
  • PRECIP: Yearly average precipitation by state in inches

Overall, we have noticed that there is a visible correlation between temperature, precipitation, and crime rates, but not necessarily FEMA declarations. The majority of this data appears in the Southeastern United States, which naturally have a higher average temperature and preciptation rate due to their geographic location.

Natural Disasters and Crime

There are been many different opinions on whether natural disasters increase or decrease crime rates in areas where they strike. Use of sociology and criminology are areas often used to asses this relationship in the wake of natural disaster; we chose to explore the spatial relationship between the two. Since the timing of the disaster and when crime rates are analyzed can play a factor, our project opted to look at the amount of natural disasters in areas of the United States and how it was spatially related to county crime rates. Portraying this data spatially helps the user see any relationship between the two categories without having to explore specific instances of disasters or crimes occurring.

References: Prelog, Andrew J. (2015). Modeling the Relationship between Natural Disasters and Crime in the United States. Nat. Hazards Rev., 2016, 17(1): 04015011. doi: http://dx.doi.org/10.1061/(ASCE)NH.1527-6996.0000190
